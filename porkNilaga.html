Pork Nilaga

A classic Filipino boiled pork soup that is both comforting and nutritious. This version features tender pork belly simmered in a clear, flavorful broth with potatoes, saba bananas, and pechay, making it the perfect meal for cold or rainy days.

Hearty pork belly and fresh vegetables in a warm, savory clear broth.
1 HR 5 MINS Total Time
Easy Difficulty
5 People Serves

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs. pork belly, cut into cubes
  • 1 Knorr Pork Cube
  • 3 to 4 small potatoes, peeled and halved
  • 2 pieces ripe saba banana, sliced
  • 6 to 8 ounces pechay (or bok choy)
  • 1 medium yellow onion, sliced
  • 6 to 7 cups water
  • 1 tablespoon whole peppercorn
  • 2 to 3 tablespoons fish sauce

Cook

1
Pour water in a cooking pot. Let it boil. Add the onion and whole peppercorns, then continue to boil until the onion becomes tender.
2
Put the pork belly into the cooking pot. Once the water re-boils, add the Knorr Pork Cube and stir well.
3
Cover the pot and simmerCooked gently in liquid just below the boiling point to soften and absorb flavors. on low heat for 45 to 60 minutes, or until the pork becomes tender.
4
Add the potatoes and saba bananas. Cook for 5 to 7 minutes until tender.
5
Stir in the fish sauce and pechay. Cook for another 3 to 5 minutes. Transfer to a bowl and serve hot with fish sauce and chili on the side!

The Warmth of Filipino Home Cooking

Nilaga, which literally means "boiled," is the ultimate comfort food in the Philippines. While it seems effortless, the secret lies in simmering the meat until it is melt-in-your-mouth tender, allowing the natural juices to meld with the sweetness of saba bananas and the earthiness of the vegetables.

The Saba Secret

Adding ripe saba bananas (plantains) provides a unique, subtle sweetness to the broth that balances the savory saltiness of the fish sauce.

Low and Slow

Always cook the meat on low heat once boiling. This prevents the broth from becoming cloudy and ensures the pork stays juicy rather than tough.

Best Served With...

Nilaga is best enjoyed with a side of "patis" (fish sauce) and calamansi or siling labuyo for an extra kick of flavor.
