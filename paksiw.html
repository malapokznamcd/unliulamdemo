Paksiw na Bangus

A quintessential Filipino dish that celebrates bold, tangy flavors. Fresh milkfish (bangus) is gently stewed in a vinegar-based broth with ginger, garlic, and onions. This version is elevated with ampalaya (bitter melon) and eggplant, creating a perfectly balanced sour and savory meal.

A Tangy Tradition

Paksiw is a Filipino style of cooking whose name means to cook and simmer in vinegar. Historically, this method was used as a natural way to preserve food in the tropical heat of the Philippines. The vinegar's acidity prevents spoilage while infusing the meat or fish with a sharp, refreshing flavor that Filipinos have come to love over generations.

The Dagupan Choice

For the best Paksiw, many chefs prefer Dagupan Bangus. These are raised in brackish water and are prized for their thick, flavorful belly fat.

Vinegar Rule

Traditional cooks advise not to stir the pot immediately after adding vinegar. Letting it boil undisturbed allows the harsh acidity to mellow out into a pleasant tang.

Bitter Balance

The addition of ampalaya (bitter melon) provides a contrast to the sour broth. To reduce bitterness, you can pre-soak the slices in salted water.
