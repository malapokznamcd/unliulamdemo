Kare-Kare (Beef Tripe)

A classic Filipino stew known for its thick, savory peanut sauce. This version uses tender beef tripe and a medley of vegetables, perfectly complemented by salty shrimp paste.

Rich and creamy peanut stew with beef tripe and vegetables.
1 HR 15 MINS Total Time
Intermediate Difficulty
6 People Serves

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs. beef tripe, sliced
  • 1 piece Knorr Beef Cube
  • 1 bunch baby bok choy
  • 15 pieces snake beans (sitaw), cut 2" long
  • 1 piece Chinese eggplant, sliced
  • 1 ½ cups peanut (processed until smooth)
  • ½ cup annatto seeds (atsuete)
  • 1 piece onion, minced
  • 5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 ½ tablespoons glutinous rice flour
  • 6 cups water
  • 3 tablespoons cooking oil
  • Ground black pepper and bagoong alamang

Cook

1
Boil the tripeThe edible lining of the stomach of various farm animals, mainly cattle, characterized by its rubbery texture and honeycomb appearance. in 1 1/2 quarts of water for 1 hour or until tender. Discard the water and set the tripe aside.
2
Prepare annatto water by boiling annatto seeds in 1 1/2 cups water for 2 minutes. Strain and set the orange water aside.
3
Sauté garlic and onion in a pot. Add the tenderized tripe and cook for 1 minute.
4
Pour in the annatto water (strained) and remaining water. Add the Knorr Beef Cube and bring to a boil.
5
Stir in the peanut paste and simmerCooked gently in liquid just below the boiling point to soften and absorb flavors. for 8 minutes. Add the rice flour slurry (mixed with 1/4 cup water) to thicken the sauce.
6
Add the eggplant and snake beans. Cook for 5 minutes. Finally, add the bok choy and cook for 1 minute. Season with pepper and serve with bagoong.

The Art of Kare-Kare

Kare-Kare is a celebrated Filipino stew that dates back centuries. While its exact origins are a mix of Indian, Malay, and Moro influences, it has become a cornerstone of Filipino celebratory dining. Its signature thick peanut sauce and vibrant orange hue make it instantly recognizable.

Natural Color

The iconic orange color comes from annatto seeds (atsuete), a natural food coloring that adds a very subtle earthy flavor.

The Bagoong Factor

Because the stew itself is traditionally prepared with little to no salt, the salty, fermented shrimp paste (bagoong) is essential to balance the richness of the peanuts.

Thickening Secret

Toasted ground rice is the traditional thickener, though many modern recipes use glutinous rice flour or cornstarch for convenience.
