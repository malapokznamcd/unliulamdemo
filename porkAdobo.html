Classic Pork Adobo

The unofficial national dish of the Philippines. This version features tender pork belly marinated in a savory and tangy blend of soy sauce, vinegar, and plenty of garlic. It is a simple yet flavorful stew that gets even better the next day.

Savory, tangy, and garlic-infused—the ultimate Filipino comfort food.
1 HOUR Cooking Time
Easy Difficulty
4 People Serves

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs pork belly (sliced into bite-sized pieces)
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1/3 cup white vinegar
  • 1 head garlic (crushed)
  • 1 tsp whole peppercorn
  • 5 pieces dried bay leaves
  • 1 cup water
  • 2 tbsp cooking oil
  • 1 tsp sugar (optional)

Pork

Cook

1
Combine pork, soy sauce, and garlic in a bowl. MarinateTo soak food in a seasoned liquid (acid, oil, and aromatics) to tenderize the texture and infuse deep flavor. for at least 30 minutes (or longer for better flavor).
2
Heat oil in a pan. Pan-fry the marinated pork (reserve the liquid) until the meat is lightly browned on all sides.
3
Pour in the remaining MarinadeA seasoned liquid—typically containing an acid, oil, and aromatics—used to soak food to enhance flavor and tenderness. and water. Add the whole peppercorns and dried bay leaves. Bring to a boil.
4
Simmer for 40 to 60 minutes on low heat until the pork is tender and the sauce has reduced.
5
Add the vinegar. Let it boil for 2-3 minutes without stirring. Add sugar if desired, then serve with hot rice!

An Ancient Tradition

Long before the Spanish arrived, indigenous Filipinos were already cooking meat with vinegar and salt to keep it from spoiling in the tropical heat. The Spanish saw this method and called it "Adobo" (from *adobar*, meaning to marinate). Later, Chinese traders introduced soy sauce, creating the dark, savory version we love today.

No Single Recipe

There are over 100 versions of Adobo! Some use coconut milk (Adobo sa Gata), while "Adobong Puti" uses only vinegar and salt.

Better Tomorrow

Adobo is famous for being "the dish that tastes better the next day" because the meat continues to absorb the marinade over time.
