Ukoy Dulong

A crunchy Filipino favorite featuring tiny silver fish (dulong) and mung bean sprouts bound in a light, seasoned batter. These golden-brown fritters are a popular snack or side dish, offering a satisfying crunch and a burst of savory flavor in every bite.

Crispy, golden-brown fish fritters made with fresh dulong and sprouts.
20 MINS Total Time
Easy Difficulty
4 People Serves

Ingredients

  • 4 oz. dulong (silver fish)
  • 2 cups mung bean sprouts (toge)
  • 1/2 cup cornstarch
  • 1/2 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 tsp baking powder
  • 1 egg
  • 1 cup water
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/2 tsp ground black pepper
  • 1/4 tsp garlic powder
  • 1/4 tsp onion powder
  • 1 1/2 cups cooking oil

Heat oil in a pan over medium heat. Measure about 1/2 cup of the mixture onto a saucer or large spoon.

Carefully slide the mixture into the hot oil. Fry until the bottom is golden brown and crisp, then flip and cook the other side.

Drain on paper towels. Serve immediately while hot with a side of spicy vinegar (sinamak or pinakurat).

A Coastal Snack

Ukoy (or okoy) is a traditional Filipino fritter typically made with small shrimp. In coastal regions or local markets where Dulong (tiny silver fish) is abundant, this version became a popular alternative. These fish are so small—roughly 1/10th the size of an anchovy—that they can be eaten whole, providing a delicate yet distinct seafood flavor that pairs perfectly with the crunch of sprouts.

The Dulong Advantage

Because the fish are tiny, they cook almost instantly and become incredibly crispy. There is no need for deboning or intensive cleaning, making it a very convenient protein source.

Maintaining Crunch

Using a combination of flour and cornstarch is the secret to a long-lasting crunch. The cornstarch prevents the batter from becoming too bread-like or soggy.

The Vinegar Pair

Fried dishes in the Philippines are almost always served with a vinegar dip. The acidity cuts through the oiliness of the fritter and enhances the natural savoriness of the fish.
